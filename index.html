Responsive Web Design

How we quicky adapt to be user-friendly while staying the least bitter.

It began with a canvas...

A literal canvas. That is how Ethan Marcotte introduced us to the idea of Responsive Web Design in his book, Responsive Web Design. Because web design is an art form and art needs a canvas for its medium. We will pretend this is all objective and save us from hours of critiquing surrealism and the idea that "everything is art." Moving on...

We have to occasionally remind ourselves that the internet hasn't been around for that long (I'm referring to functional internet - complete with search engines and full-spectrum color). In addition, the number of different-sized devices that we use to access the internet have exponentially increased within the last few years as well. The price we pay for trying to be *unique* and *original*. But what does that mean? It means a headache for front-end developers when attempting to design and deliver a wonderful viewing experience. At least decent, on all of those canvases.

Responsive web design introduces the idea that we can satisfy all of our users with their *unique* and *original* devices with minimal alterations to our original code. Meaning, your beautiful baby of a website will look as pretty and be as functional on your desktop computer as it will on your phone or tablet. It lets the designer ease off of fixed-width constrictions without losing control. Yes, we're all control freaks. But there is a bonus! It also allows you to say you can easily adapt to change because change is good- add that to your LinkedIn! All you'll need to do is include a little of that math you swore off of after college.
